When does equity make sense?

No collateral yet.

If the facility isn't built, you can't pledge it. Pre-revenue companies often can't qualify for equipment financing or project debt. Equity fills the gap before assets exist.

Absorbs downside risk.

If the project fails, there's no loan to repay. Debt on a failed project is brutal. Equity investors share the loss.

Not everything is an asset.

R&D, hiring, regulatory approvals, working capital during buildout — these aren't things a lender will underwrite. Equity covers the gaps.

So what's the point?

Know the effective cost of each piece. Don't use 44% equity to buy a machine you could lease at 10%.

r = (E / (V + A))^(1/T) − 1 A = Amount raised V = Pre-money valuation E = Valuation at exit T = Years to exit V + A = Post-money valuation

When you raise $A at a pre-money valuation of $V, investors own A/(V+A) of the company. If the company reaches value $E after T years, the investor's share is worth E×A/(V+A). The annualized rate of return on their investment — which is your effective interest rate on that capital — is the rate that satisfies A×(1+r)T = E×A/(V+A). That simplifies to r = (E/(V+A))^(1/T) − 1. This is the investor's IRR, and it's what you're paying for the money.
